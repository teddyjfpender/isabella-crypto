Lattice Cryptography

Understanding the mathematical foundations of post-quantum security.

Introduction

Lattice-based cryptography is a family of cryptographic constructions whose security relies on the presumed hardness of certain mathematical problems on lattices. These problems are believed to be resistant to attacks by both classical and quantum computers, making lattice cryptography a leading candidate for post-quantum security.

>
Why Lattices?

Unlike RSA and elliptic curve cryptography, no efficient quantum algorithm is known for solving lattice problems. This makes them ideal for long-term security.

What are Lattices?

A lattice is a discrete subgroup of R^n that spans the entire space. Intuitively, think of it as an infinite grid of regularly spaced points in n-dimensional space.

definition 
Given linearly independent vectors b1, b2, ..., bn in R^n,
the lattice L generated by these vectors is:

    L(b1, ..., bn) = { sum(ai * bi) | ai in Z }

The vectors {b1, ..., bn} form a basis for L.

The same lattice can have many different bases. Finding a "good" basis (one with short, nearly orthogonal vectors) from a "bad" basis is computationally hard.

Hard Problems

The security of lattice cryptography rests on the computational difficulty of several related problems:

Shortest Vector Problem (SVP)

Given a lattice basis, find the shortest non-zero vector in the lattice. This problem is NP-hard under randomized reductions.

Closest Vector Problem (CVP)

Given a lattice basis and a target point, find the lattice point closest to the target. CVP is at least as hard as SVP.

Learning With Errors (LWE)

Distinguish between random samples and samples that encode a secret with small noise. Reducible to worst-case lattice problems.

Learning With Errors

The LWE problem, introduced by Oded Regev in 2005, is the foundation for many lattice-based encryption schemes, including the one implemented in Isabella.

lwe_problem 
-- LWE Distribution
Given:
  - Secret vector s in Z_q^n
  - Random matrix A in Z_q^(m x n)
  - Small error vector e in Z_q^m

Compute:
  b = A * s + e  (mod q)

Problem:
  Distinguish (A, b) from uniform random (A, u)

The key insight is that the small error term e makes it computationally infeasible to recover s from the noisy linear equations, even given many samples.

Ring-LWE

Ring-LWE is a structured variant of LWE that operates over polynomial rings. It offers significant efficiency improvements while maintaining strong security guarantees.

Property LWE Ring-LWE
Key Size O(n^2) O(n)
Operations Matrix mult Polynomial mult
Security Worst-case Ideal lattices

Applications

Lattice-based constructions enable a wide variety of cryptographic applications:

  • Public-key encryption - Secure communication against quantum attackers
  • Digital signatures - NIST standardized Dilithium and Falcon
  • Fully homomorphic encryption - Compute on encrypted data
  • Identity-based encryption - Use identities as public keys
  • Attribute-based encryption - Fine-grained access control
